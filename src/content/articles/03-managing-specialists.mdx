Nobody warned me about the particular flavour of awkwardness that comes from becoming a manager of a craft you love. You think — I'll still design, just with more meetings. You won't. Or, you will, but it will be different work than the work you left behind.

## The thing about taste

When you're a senior IC, your taste is your contribution. You see the problem; you know the shape of the answer. You push back on the brief. You ship.

When you're managing other ICs, your taste is still there, but it has to stand one step back. You can point. You can ask questions. You cannot, and must not, sketch over someone else's work to show them what you meant. The short-term gain — a better artifact — is purchased with a long-term loss: a junior who learns to guess at your taste instead of building their own.

## The second chair

There is a musician's metaphor that helped me. If you were first chair, and you move to conduct, you do not get better by holding on to your instrument. You get better by hearing what every chair in the room is playing, and saying only the smallest thing that will help that player sound more like themselves.

The first chair trusts their ears. The conductor trusts everyone else's.

> The skill that gets you promoted is rarely the skill that does the next job. The mistake is using it anyway, because it is the one you know.

## What helped me

- A weekly 1:1 that is theirs, not mine. They bring the agenda. I bring the time.
- Asking "what do you think is the strongest version of this?" before saying what *I* think.
- Being willing to ship a 7/10 version of something that is their call, rather than an 8/10 version that is mine.
- Learning to spend an hour on the design, then closing the file and letting it go.

None of that is clever. All of it is uncomfortable. The discomfort, I think, is the point — it's where the old instinct is retraining itself to be useful in a different key.
