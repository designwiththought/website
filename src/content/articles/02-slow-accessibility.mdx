For a long time I thought accessibility was a series of discrete problems you could knock off a list. A focus style here. An aria-label there. A missing tab stop. Ship, close the ticket, move on.

The problem with that model is not that it's wrong. It's that it's too fast. Accessibility work, done well, is slow work — the kind that requires sitting with a product for long enough to notice the second-order effects.

## What fast-accessibility misses

The modal that's keyboard-accessible *and also* traps focus *and also* announces itself *and also* doesn't reopen at the wrong time *and also* doesn't interrupt a live region. Each of those is a one-line fix. The interaction they produce together is a month of research.

Fast accessibility ships a modal that "passes." Slow accessibility ships a modal that a blind colleague uses without saying anything about it afterwards, because nothing went wrong.

## A short case for patience

We don't celebrate this kind of work enough. It's invisible when it goes well. The only time anyone notices is when it doesn't. But that's also, I think, what makes it worth doing — the measure of success is that no one is forced to think about the software, which is, more often than not, the whole point of the software.
