<Dropcap text="Six years ago I was shipping accessibility fixes in week-long sprints and calling it done. We'd pick a surface, run a scan, fix the top issues, move on. It felt disciplined. It looked good on a roadmap." />

It was, I think now, almost entirely the wrong shape of work.

## The sprint trap

A sprint is a good container for a known problem. You can scope it, size it, demo it. Accessibility doesn't usually fit that container. Most of the interesting bugs are not in one component — they are in the *relationship* between components, or between components and the surrounding page, or between a component and a particular assistive technology that does something unexpected.

You cannot close those bugs in a week because you cannot *see* them in a week. You need to live inside the product with a screen reader open, for long enough that the gentle, persistent discomforts stop feeling like noise and start feeling like data.

<PullquoteNoCite text="The first month with NVDA always sounds like a fire alarm. The sixth month sounds like a colleague, politely listing what you&rsquo;ve broken." />

## What the work wants

Accessibility work wants a longer pulse. It wants someone to be slightly bored of a problem before they fix it, because boredom is what you feel after you've reproduced the issue enough times to know what's actually happening.

It wants room for the second fix. The first fix makes the scanner quiet; the second fix makes the screen reader make sense. The gap between those is often where the craft lives.

## A slower cadence

At Superhuman we switched to quarter-length investigations for the harder surfaces. Each investigation starts with a week of "what does it sound like?" — no fixes, no tickets, just notes. The fixes come later and are usually smaller than we expected. The understanding, every time, is larger.

I don't think this is a universal answer. But I am certain it is a better starting question than "what can we close this sprint?"
