Most teams treat `aria-live` as a plumbing decision. You pick the region, you decide between *polite* and *assertive*, you wire it up. The copy inside the region — the actual announcement a user hears — is usually an afterthought, written to whatever string was handy at the time.

Over six weeks, I timed and transcribed 240 announcements across six products. The surface pattern was clear: the products with good reputations for accessibility had announcements that read like a tactful colleague. The products with bad reputations had announcements that read like a server log. The craft gap was not in the wiring. It was in the writing.

## The method

I ran each product through a short scripted session — send a message, delete a message, apply a filter, open a modal — with VoiceOver on macOS and NVDA on Windows. I recorded every utterance, timed it in seconds, and captured the corresponding HTML and ARIA so I could reconstruct why the screen reader had said what it said.

Announcements fell into three broad buckets:

- **Narrative** — "Message sent to Priya. Now in Sent." Reads like a colleague describing what happened.
- **Mechanical** — "alert: message.sent: true" or "Snackbar: message_sent". Reads like a log line leaking out of the app.
- **Redundant** — re-announcing the element you already have focus on, or repeating a label because two live regions both fired.

Of the 240 announcements I captured, 61 were narrative, 104 were mechanical, and 75 were redundant or partially redundant. The products people liked using skewed narrative; the products people tolerated skewed mechanical.

## The pattern

The narrative announcements shared four small habits:

1. **Verb first, subject known.** "Sent to Priya." Not "Message to Priya was sent."
2. **Short.** Six to twelve words. Anything longer competes with the next action.
3. **Names over nouns.** "Deleted the April invoice." Not "Deleted the item."
4. **Quiet on success, loud on change of state.** If nothing surprising happened, the announcement was a receipt, not a fanfare.

> The best announcement I recorded was four words long and told me my draft had been saved. The worst was thirty-one words long and told me the same thing in a tone that suggested legal liability.

## The fix

You don't need to rearchitect your ARIA to get this right. You need an editor in the loop. On the Superhuman team we now run every live-region string through the same review process as our UI copy — short brief, writing partner, read-aloud check, ship. The wiring stays where it is; the words get the attention they always needed.

The next time you add an `aria-live` region, write the announcement out loud before you write it in code. If you can hear yourself saying it, it will probably read like prose. If you can't, rewrite.
