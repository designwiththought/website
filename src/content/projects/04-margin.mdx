## Premise

Digital reading tools treat annotation as a second-class citizen. Highlighting is a lasso. Comments are a sidebar. Notes are another app. The thing you actually do with a good book — you draw arrows, you underline unevenly, you write "hmm" in the margin — has no counterpart in the software.

Margin was a prototype that asked: what if the margin were a first-class surface, with its own typography, its own gestures, its own rules?

## What it did

- A two-column long-form reader, with the body on the left and a generous margin on the right.
- Marginalia — handwritten-feeling notes — that could be drawn, typed, or pulled from a short list of glyphs (arrow, circle, underline, question).
- A "quiet" mode that faded the body text and brought the marginalia to the front, for when you were re-reading what *you* had said.
- An export that preserved the annotations as a footnoted second document.

## Why it's paused

Two reasons. The first is that the interaction was good but the retention was not — people tried it once, made interesting marks, and then did not come back. A reader is not a habit unless it is also a library, and I did not want to build a library.

The second is that a colleague's team at Superhuman is working on something related, and I'd rather help them than duplicate the work. The prototype source is public; if it inspires something, that is enough.
